# 💰 Salary Negotiation — Get to 20 LPA

> **The biggest salary jumps happen in 30-minute conversations. Not in 3 years of work.**
> **Most engineers under-negotiate by 20-40%. Don't.**

---

## 🎯 The mindset shift

- **You are not "lucky to be interviewed."** They NEEDED someone. You are solving their problem.
- **They lowball by 15-20%** and expect you to counter. It's a dance.
- **The first number anchors the negotiation.** Whoever says it first usually loses.
- **You don't need the job.** (Or at least, you should feel that way.)

---

## 🏆 The golden rules

### 1. **Never name your number first.**
**Them:** "What are your salary expectations?"
**You:** "I'm flexible on the exact number — what is the budgeted range for this role?"

If they push:
- "I'm focused on finding the right role. I've seen this kind of role go for 22-28 LPA in the market. But I want to understand the full comp package before we get into specifics."

### 2. **Always have competing offers.**
- The single biggest leverage in negotiation.
- Apply to 5+ companies in parallel. Don't disclose offer details until asked.
- 2+ offers = 30-50% higher final offer.

### 3. **Total comp > base salary**
- Base, stocks (RSU/ESOP), signing bonus, relocation, learning budget, WFH stipend
- A 22 LPA with 5 LPA stocks > 25 LPA cash. Stocks have upside.
- Don't just negotiate base. Negotiate the whole package.

### 4. **Always counter.**
- They offer 18? Counter with 24. They'll come back at 21-22.
- The first offer is never the final offer. **Never.**

### 5. **Be ready to walk away.**
- If they won't meet your floor, politely decline.
- "I appreciate the offer, but I need to think about whether this aligns with my goals. Can we revisit the package?"
- Walking away often gets them to call back with a better offer.

### 6. **Never lie or bluff.**
- Don't fake offers. Recruiters talk.
- Don't lie about current CTC. They verify with offer letters.
- "I'm currently at X, looking for Y to Z" is fine. Don't inflate.

## 🗣️ Scripts that work

### When they ask your expected salary (early call):

> "I'd love to learn more about the role first. Could you share the budgeted range? I want to make sure we're aligned before going deeper."

If they really push:
> "Based on my research and the market for AI Engineers with my skills and experience, I'm targeting the 22-26 LPA range total compensation. But I'm flexible depending on the role, growth, and full package."

### When they give you a lowball offer:

> "Thank you for the offer. I'm really excited about the role and the team. That said, the base is below what I was expecting based on my research and the market. Is there flexibility on the number?"

If they say "no":
> "I understand. Could we look at other levers — like an early performance review at 6 months with a salary bump to X, additional stock grant, or a signing bonus?"

### When you have a competing offer (the killer):

> "I want to be transparent — I'm in late stages with another company at [higher number]. I'm genuinely more interested in this role because of [reason]. Can you help me choose you by closing the gap?"

This is **the most powerful sentence in negotiation**. Use it carefully and only if true.

### When you're at the limit:

> "I really want to make this work. Can we split the difference and meet at [middle ground]? That feels like a number I can enthusiastically accept."

### The "yes":

> "I'm excited to accept. Thank you for the partnership. What's the next step?"

Don't re-negotiate after they say yes. That's a rescinded offer waiting to happen.

## 💡 Pro techniques

### The "flinch"
When they give the number, look slightly disappointed. Pause. "Hmm." Then say thank you and move on. This signals: I'm not jumping for joy, you may need to come up.

### The "let me think about it"
Never accept on the call. Always say: "I'm excited, let me review the full package and get back to you by [date]." This is standard, they expect it, and it gives you time to think.

### The "comp package" pivot
If base is firm, ask for:
- More equity/stocks
- Signing bonus (one-time, less risky for them)
- Early performance review (with raise)
- Remote work flexibility (worth ~2-3 LPA)
- Learning budget (courses, conferences)
- Extra PTO
- Title bump (Staff, Senior, etc.)
- Joining bonus

### The "compete with my current" (if you're underpaid)
If your current is 12 LPA and they offer 18:
> "I appreciate the 50% jump — that's significant. Based on my skills and the market, I was hoping for 22+. Is there room to close the gap?"

If they say 18 is the max:
> "Could we structure it as 18 base + 4 LPA joining bonus + 6-month review to 22?"

## 📝 Things to ask before accepting

1. **Base salary** — exact number
2. **Stocks/RSU/ESOP** — vesting schedule, current value, exit scenarios
3. **Signing bonus** — when paid, clawback terms
4. **Variable/performance bonus** — what's the target, how is it measured
5. **Health insurance** — coverage for family?
6. **WFH policy** — full remote, hybrid, in-office?
7. **Leave policy** — sick, casual, earned
8. **Relocation** — if relevant
9. **Learning budget** — courses, books, conferences
10. **Equipment** — laptop, monitor, home office stipend
11. **Title** — Senior, Staff, etc.
12. **Reporting structure** — who do you report to, team size
13. **Review cycle** — when's the first performance review
14. **Probation period** — length, conditions

## 🚩 Red flags in an offer

Walk away or push back if:
- ❌ They won't share the comp range
- ❌ They pressure you to accept fast ("sign today or we rescind")
- ❌ Stocks have a 5-year vest with no liquidity event
- ❌ No clarity on bonus structure
- ❌ Base is way below market (15%+ below)
- ❌ Title is "Engineer II" when peers are "Senior"
- ❌ They get offended when you negotiate (huge red flag about culture)
- ❌ No mention of team, manager, or growth

- "If I don't ask, I don't get. The worst they say is 'no.'"

## 📅 The negotiation timeline

```
Day 0:    Receive verbal offer
Day 1:    "Thank you, I'll review and get back by [Day 3]"
Day 2:    Research market (levels.fyi, Blind, recruiter chats)
Day 3:    Counter with your number (use scripts above)
Day 4-7:  Iterate 1-2 rounds
Day 7-10: Final offer
Day 10:   Accept or decline
```

**Never accept the first offer. Always take at least 24 hours.**

---

## 🎯 TL;DR — The 7-step negotiation

1. Research market comp first (levels.fyi, recruiters)
2. Get competing offers if possible
3. Never name your number first
4. Always counter (aim for 20%+ above their first)
5. Negotiate total comp, not just base
6. Be ready to walk away (mentally)
7. Accept graciously when you have what you need
